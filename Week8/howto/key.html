API Keys

What is it, and how do I get one?

I mentioned previously that many APIs are offered up as free services. The Guardian's API is one such service. But that doesn't mean there aren't strings attached. Developers at these companies incur a cost for maintaining these free services in the form of potentially millions of requests for data. And thus, these developers have devised method to monitor and control access to the service. For our purposes, that method is the use of an API key.

Fortunately, obtaining such a key is quite easy. You just need to sign up for a free developer account.

After you've signed up, check your email for your very own API key. It should look something like the following: 83a8e081-5935-45db-c766-043967bb1ec3

Before we move on to actually using your new key, a quick bit of advice from Cat Gandalf:

If your key gets into the wrong hands, it could be used to make requests on your behalf. This could result in a violation of the API's terms of service and a full ban from the service. So: don't give away your key, don't post it online, and don't accidentally include it in a public repository like GitHub. Folks can and do write scripts that troll public repositories for exposed keys. But enough of the scary talk, let's use our key!
